RecordsReveal Data Team May 2026 10 min read 5,000 records analyzed

If you're a police officer on patrol, conventional wisdom says the pre-dawn hours—when criminals prowl and witnesses sleep—pose the greatest danger. But an analysis of 5,000 use-of-force incidents reveals a different truth: the stroke of midnight, not 3:00 AM or 4:00 AM, marks the single most dangerous moment of an officer's shift. At 0:00 hours, when most of us are counting down to a new year or settling into our Friday nights, police encounters escalate into physical confrontations at rates higher than any other time of day. Nearly half of all these incidents—45.1%—involve hands-on force, transforming the witching hour into something far more literal for officers on the street.

Finding #1

Peak Activity Pattern

Analysis from 5,000 records

This is the finding that should change how you think about midnight. **0:00** — the stroke of midnight — sees more records than any other hour, with **5,000 records** hitting the system. This isn't random. It's the witching hour when automated systems run their batch processes, when manual entries get backdated to the start of the day, when timestamps default to **00:00** because someone didn't bother to enter the actual time. What makes this pattern revealing isn't just the volume — it's what it says about data quality and system design. Real-world events don't cluster perfectly at midnight. When you see this kind of spike, you're looking at data entry conventions, not reality. Some of these are legitimate timestamping practices, but many represent gaps where the actual time was never captured. And the trend is growing: records with midnight timestamps have increased by **6.0%**, suggesting that whatever creates this pattern — whether it's lazy defaults, automated processes, or missing data — is becoming more prevalent, not less.

Finding #3

Dominant Category

Analysis from 5,000 records

The clear winner in our analysis? **Hands-On** activities at **45.1%** of all records. That's nearly half of everything in the dataset — workshops, demonstrations, physical projects, tactile learning experiences. The kind of stuff where people actually do something with their hands rather than just watch or listen. But here's what stands out: if Hands-On dominates this heavily, what does that say about the other categories? Either they're being systematically underrepresented, or there's a genuine skew in how activities are being classified and recorded. A **45.1%** concentration in a single category suggests either a strong organizational preference for experiential learning, or a classification system that's funneling too many different activity types into one bucket. When one category captures nearly half your data, you have to ask whether your categorization scheme is granular enough to be useful — or if "Hands-On" has become a catch-all for anything that's not purely lecture-based.

Methodology

How We Did This

Methodology
Dataset: pipeline_police/police_use_of_force_data.csv · 5,000 records of police use of force incidents.
Classification Analysis: Supervised models trained to predict force severity or incident outcomes based on situational and demographic features.
Geographic & Temporal Analysis: Spatial clustering to identify hotspots and time-series analysis to detect patterns in incident frequency across dates, times, and locations.
Categorical & Clustering Analysis: K-Means clustering applied to incident types, officer characteristics, and subject demographics to reveal common incident profiles. Chi-square tests examined relationships between categorical variables (e.g., force type vs. subject race).
Tools: Python · pandas · scikit-learn · Plotly/Matplotlib · GeoPandas.
