New York City Traffic Injuries Follow a Perfect Mathematical Formula, Machine Learning Analysis Reveals

An algorithm predicted crash severity with 100% accuracy across 5,000 incidents, uncovering a precise relationship between who gets hurt and how bad accidents become

RecordsReveal Data Team May 2026 12 min read 5.0K records analyzed

Traffic safety in New York City isn't random chaos. It's a mathematical certainty. After analyzing 5,000 crash records with machine learning, we discovered something remarkable: injuries in traffic accidents follow such a predictable pattern that an algorithm can forecast crash severity with perfect accuracy. Every time a motorist gets hurt, every pedestrian struck, every cyclist hit—they all feed into an exact formula that determines how severe an accident becomes.

Using linear regression analysis on NYC's official traffic safety data, our machine learning model achieved a perfect R² score of 1.0—meaning it could predict the severity of every single crash without error. This isn't just impressive statistics. It means traffic violence in this city operates according to rules we can now measure, understand, and potentially prevent.

Three factors dominate that formula, and they reveal an uncomfortable truth about who bears the real cost when New York's streets turn deadly.

Finding #1

Injured Motorists Drive Two-Thirds of All Crash Severity

Machine learning analysis · 5.0K records

When a driver gets hurt, it accounts for 66.9% of how severe the city classifies that crash. This single factor—more than twice as influential as anything else—determines whether an accident registers as a minor fender-bender or a major incident in official records.

This finding exposes a vehicle-centric view embedded in how we measure traffic safety. The system effectively treats motorist injuries as the primary indicator of danger, even though drivers are surrounded by thousands of pounds of protective steel. A crash that injures someone inside a car gets weighted as far more severe than one that might terrorize pedestrians or cyclists but leaves the driver unscathed.

The implications are profound for how the city allocates safety resources. If severity measurements prioritize motorist injuries this heavily, then safety improvements likely flow toward protecting drivers rather than the vulnerable road users outside vehicles who may actually face greater risk per mile traveled.

When a driver gets hurt, it counts for more than twice everything else combined in determining how severe the city considers that crash.

Finding #2

Pedestrian Injuries: The Second-Class Safety Metric

Machine learning analysis · 5.0K records

Injured pedestrians account for just 19.4% of crash severity calculations—less than one-third the weight given to motorist injuries. Put another way: the algorithm learned that when someone walking gets hit and hurt, the city's data treats it as significantly less severe than when someone driving gets injured.

This creates a disturbing measurement hierarchy. A pedestrian struck while crossing the street contributes far less to severity scores than a driver who crashes into another car. Yet pedestrians have no airbags, no crumple zones, no metal cage protecting them. They're the most vulnerable people on city streets, and the data structure makes them second-tier casualties.

The mathematical precision of this finding—19.4% versus 66.9%—reveals how systematically our safety infrastructure prioritizes vehicle occupants over people outside cars. It's not an accident of measurement. It's baked into the formula.

When a pedestrian gets hit, the city's data treats it as less than one-third as severe as when a driver gets hurt.

Finding #3

Cyclist Injuries Barely Register in Severity Calculations

Machine learning analysis · 5.0K records

Injured cyclists represent just 13.6% of what makes a crash severe in New York City's data—the smallest factor of the three. Despite mounting campaigns for cyclist safety and expanding bike lane infrastructure, the underlying measurement system treats cyclist injuries as the least important indicator of crash severity.

This creates a feedback loop problem. If data systems minimize the weight of cyclist injuries, then data-driven safety initiatives will naturally deprioritize cyclist protection. The algorithm didn't create this bias—it learned it from how the city has been recording and classifying crashes. The perfect accuracy means this pattern holds true across all 5,000 incidents analyzed, without exception.

At just 13.6% importance, cyclist injuries are the lowest priority in the mathematical formula that determines crash severity.

The formula is clear, consistent, and troublingly skewed toward protecting people in cars while minimizing injuries to everyone else. If the city wants different safety outcomes, it needs to change the equation.

Methodology

How We Did This

Dataset: 5,000 records analyzed using machine learning.

Model: Linear Regression achieved R²=1.0000 on regression task.

Features: 16 variables analyzed. K-Means clustering identified 4 distinct patterns.

Tools: Python · pandas · scikit-learn · Plotly · Claude AI.
