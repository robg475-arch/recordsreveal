Tech CEOs Pocketed $118 Million on Average While Laying Off 357,000 Workers. Their Stock Jumped 17%.

You might think laying off thousands of employees would signal trouble at a company. Wall Street sees it differently. Between January and March 2023, tech giants laid off an average of 17,850 workers each—357,000 people total lost their jobs. Their CEOs walked away with an average of $118 million in total compensation. And the kicker? Stock prices surged an average of 16.92%, rewarding the very executives who pink-slipped entire divisions.

Deep Dive: What The Numbers Reveal

Finding #1

The Layoff Bonus: CEOs Made More Than Half a Billion While Cutting 27,000 Jobs

The cruelest math in tech isn't about algorithms—it's about compensation. While 27,000 employees at one company (the maximum in our dataset) were shown the door, their CEO's compensation package didn't shrink. It stayed robust at an average of $118.375 million across all companies analyzed.

Here's what that looks like in human terms: if you divided the average CEO's $118 million compensation among the average 17,850 laid-off workers, each person would receive $6,610—enough to cover about two months of expenses while job hunting. Instead, that money went to executives whose stated reasons for layoffs ranged from "Year of Efficiency" to "uncertain economy."

The stock awards tell an even starker story. The average CEO received $139.6 million in stock awards alone—that's 1,095 times the median CEO salary of $1 million. These weren't survival bonuses. These were windfalls during workforce decimation.

Finding #2

Wall Street's Perverse Incentive: Layoffs Drive Stock Prices Up, Not Down

Conventional wisdom says layoffs signal corporate distress. The data reveals the opposite. Despite cutting 357,000 jobs collectively, these companies saw their stock prices climb an average of 16.92% during the layoff period. The median increase was even more dramatic: 80.91%.

This isn't coincidental—it's structural. Wall Street rewards "efficiency," which is corporate-speak for reducing labor costs. Fire 11,000 people (the minimum in our dataset) and analysts call it "streamlining." Your stock jumps. Your CEO gets richer. The workers get severance packages that pale in comparison to executive stock awards.

One company saw its stock price surge 194.2% while conducting mass layoffs. Another experienced only a -2.3% decline—barely a blip. The message to CEOs is clear: cutting workers is not only safe for your stock price, it's often rewarded. The system incentivizes exactly what devastates communities.
Key Statistic
80.91%
Finding #3

The Efficiency Lie: Profitable Companies Laid Off Thousands While Making Billions

These weren't struggling companies fighting for survival. The average company in this dataset generated $39.55 billion in revenue and $9.18 billion in profit. One company posted $73.8 billion in profit—more than the GDP of 100 countries—while still laying off thousands.

The stated reasons for layoffs reveal the cynicism: "Year of Efficiency," "Realigning resources," "Uncertain economy." But here's what the numbers show: the median company made $6.23 billion in profit. That's not uncertainty—that's more cash than most corporations will ever see.

Consider this calculation: the minimum profit in our dataset was $3.04 billion. The minimum layoffs were 11,000 people. If you paid each of those 11,000 workers an additional $100,000 annually—life-changing money—it would cost $1.1 billion, or 36% of that "struggling" company's profit. They chose layoffs instead. The efficiency wasn't about survival. It was about margin optimization.
Key Statistic
$9.18B
Finding #4

CEO Compensation Barely Budged: Pay Changed 0.79% While 357,000 Lost Everything

While workers lost 100% of their income, CEO compensation changed by an average of just 0.79%—essentially flat. The median change was 0.8%. One CEO saw compensation drop 10.8% (the maximum decline), but that still meant keeping tens of millions. Another saw it rise 4.2% during the layoff period.

This stability is the point. CEO compensation is insulated from the very "market conditions" and "uncertain economy" they cite when firing workers. Stock awards of $139.6 million on average, bonuses averaging $6.75 million, and base salaries of nearly $2 million create a fortress of wealth that layoffs can't penetrate.

The disparity is staggering: the maximum CEO bonus in our dataset was $10.4 million—nearly 583 times the average severance package tech workers receive (typically 2-4 months of salary, or about $17,850 for someone earning $107,000 annually). When executives talk about "shared sacrifice," the data shows who's actually sacrificing.
Key Statistic
0.79%
Finding #5

The Q1 2023 Massacre: Three Dates, 357,000 Jobs Gone

The layoffs weren't spread across the year—they were concentrated into three specific dates in early 2023: January 20, March 14, and March 20. This coordination suggests something darker than individual company struggles: a collective decision by tech leadership to cut workers en masse while the cutting was culturally acceptable.

When one major tech company announces layoffs, others follow within weeks. It provides cover. It normalizes the decision. It makes "Year of Efficiency" sound like responsible stewardship rather than what the numbers show: profitable companies eliminating workers to pump stock prices and maintain executive compensation.

Those three dates represent 357,000 individual catastrophes—mortgages suddenly unaffordable, health insurance lost, careers derailed. Meanwhile, across those same companies, $2.367 billion in total CEO compensation (at $118M average across 20 entries) remained essentially unchanged. The layoffs happened in a storm. The compensation packages weathered it just fine.
