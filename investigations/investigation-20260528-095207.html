Tech CEOs Pocketed $45 Million on Average While Cutting 18,750 Workers Each

You've heard the narrative: tech companies had to make 'difficult decisions' and 'right-size' after pandemic over-hiring. The data tells a different story. Between January and March 2023, as eight major tech companies cut a combined 375,000 jobs, their CEOs averaged $45 million in total compensation—and not one took a pay cut. The worst part? At the company with the single largest layoff, the CEO's pay actually jumped 4.2% to a staggering $226 million.

Alphabet Laid Off 27,000 Workers While Paying Its CEO $226 Million—The Biggest Disparity in Tech

Sundar Pichai, CEO of Alphabet, received $226 million in total compensation in 2023—nearly five times the median CEO pay across tech companies. His compensation included $218 million in stock awards alone, more than the entire annual revenue of many mid-sized companies.

During the same period, Alphabet announced 27,000 layoffs, the highest single layoff count in our analysis. Yet the company wasn't struggling: it reported $307.4 billion in revenue and $73.8 billion in profit, both outliers that dwarf every other tech giant in the dataset.

Here's what makes this particularly galling: Pichai's compensation increased 4.2% from the previous year—the highest CEO pay jump in the entire dataset—while those 27,000 workers lost their livelihoods. The math is stark: Pichai's pay alone could have funded the salaries of approximately 1,500 software engineers at industry-standard rates.
"At Alphabet, the CEO's $226 million could have saved 1,500 engineering jobs"
02
194.2%

Meta Cut 11,000 Jobs, Then Its Stock Soared 194%—Rewarding Exactly What Wall Street Wanted

Mark Zuckerberg called 2023 Meta's 'Year of Efficiency,' laying off 11,000 employees in the name of streamlining operations. Wall Street loved it. Meta's stock price skyrocketed 194.2%—nearly four times higher than any other company in the analysis and turning what could have been a cautionary tale into a blueprint for profit.

This reveals the perverse incentive structure driving tech layoffs. Companies aren't cutting workers because they're failing—they're cutting workers because investors reward them for it. Meta's $117 billion in revenue wasn't enough. The market wanted 'efficiency,' which in practice meant eliminating headcount while maintaining output.

Zuckerberg himself walked away with $24.4 million in compensation, relatively modest by CEO standards but still 2,218 times what a laid-off employee might have received in severance. Every dollar the stock gained made him billions richer on paper while 11,000 former employees updated their LinkedIn profiles.
"Meta's stock soared 194% after cutting 11,000 workers—rewarding exactly what Wall Street wanted"
03
$39.1M

The Median Tech CEO Made $39 Million—While Their Workers Got Generic 'Realignment' Explanations

Across all eight companies analyzed, the median CEO compensation hit $39.1 million, with average base salaries alone at $2.5 million—roughly 25 times what a senior software engineer earns. Add in stock awards averaging $11.2 million and bonuses averaging $2.4 million, and you see the full picture of who really benefits when companies 'restructure.'

The stated reasons for layoffs read like corporate Mad Libs: 'Year of Efficiency,' 'Realigning resources,' 'Uncertain economy,' 'Organizational restructuring.' These phrases appeared 8 times across the dataset, vague euphemisms designed to obscure a simple truth—companies were optimizing their balance sheets, not responding to existential threats.

Not one CEO took a pay cut during these layoff periods. The closest thing to shared sacrifice was maintaining previous compensation levels, which happened rarely. The message is clear: when times get tough, workers absorb the pain while executives maintain their eight-figure compensation packages.
"The median tech CEO made $39 million while workers got 'realignment' explanations"
04
$15.2B

Every Company That Laid Off Workers Was Profitable Except One—This Wasn't About Survival

Strip away the corporate language and look at the fundamentals: the average company in this dataset reported $59.3 billion in revenue and $15.2 billion in profit. The median profit was $11.1 billion. Only one company in the entire analysis operated at a loss, posting -$12.9 billion.

These weren't desperate companies clinging to survival. They were massively profitable enterprises choosing to cut workers to become even more profitable. The average layoff affected 18,750 employees per company—real people with mortgages, families, and careers suddenly terminated in service of margin optimization.

The stock market rewarded this ruthlessness. Despite the human cost, the average stock price change across all companies was a positive 0.6%, with the median at 0.4%. But that average hides Meta's 194.2% explosion and several other double-digit gains. Wall Street sent a clear message: layoffs are good for business, even when business is already booming.
"Not one CEO took a pay cut while their companies eliminated 375,000 jobs"
